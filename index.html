Section 1Research Question & Theoretical Framework

Do local immigration policy choices affect how community members, donors, and government funders perceive and support immigrant-serving nonprofits?

Integrated Theoretical Framework

This study bridges three theoretical traditions to explain how policy signals travel from local governments to nonprofit stakeholders:

Foundation

Young's (2000) Government-Nonprofit Relations

Establishes three structural modes: supplementary (filling gaps), complementary (partnering), and adversarial (challenging). Explains what relationships look like but not how policies reshape them.

Behavioral Mechanism

Signaling Theory (Spence, 1973; Connelly et al., 2011)

Provides the behavioral bridge. Local governments act as signalers; immigration policies are observable signals that stakeholders decode to update beliefs about the legitimacy of immigrant-serving organizations.

Signal Content

Vedung's (1998) Policy Tool Typology

Specifies signal direction: Carrots (incentives) signal inclusion and legitimacy. Sticks (enforcement) signal restriction and exclusion. Different tools encode fundamentally different messages.

Conceptual Model

Section 2Research Hypotheses

Twelve directional hypotheses derived from the signaling framework: direct effects of policy tools (H1, H2) and the perceived NPO's role (H3), plus moderation by the NPO's role (H4).

🥕 Carrot Policies (Inclusion & Legitimacy)

H1a: Carrot policies are positively associated with government grants to immigrant-serving nonprofits.
H1b: Carrot policies are negatively associated with private donations (crowding-out).
H1c: Carrot policies are negatively associated with program service revenue (reduced fee demand).

🪨 Stick Policies (Threat & Urgency)

H2a: Stick policies are negatively associated with government grants (adversarial defunding).
H2b: Stick policies are positively associated with private donations (backlash / rage giving).
H2c: Stick policies are positively associated with program service revenue (heightened demand).

🏛️ Direct Effects of Perceived NPO's Role

Where nonprofits are recognized as primary providers of immigrant services, their structural visibility shapes revenue patterns independent of policy signals.

H3a: Perceived NPO's role is positively associated with private donations (centrality cues donor support).
H3b: Perceived NPO's role is positively associated with government grants (NPO centrality opens partnership channels).
H3c: Perceived NPO's role is negatively associated with program service revenue (substitution away from fees).

🔗 Moderation by Perceived NPO's Role

Policy signals do not operate in a vacuum: their interpretation depends on whether NPOs serve as the salient organizational target. Where NPOs are primary providers, abstract policy signals are translated into concrete signals about NPO support or NPO threat.

H4a: Perceived NPO's role moderates the relationship between immigration policies and private donations.
H4b: Perceived NPO's role moderates the relationship between immigration policies and government grants.
H4c: Perceived NPO's role moderates the relationship between immigration policies and program service revenue.

Three Dependent Variables, Three Stakeholder Mechanisms

Each DV is tied to a distinct stakeholder mechanism:

1. Private Donations — community / donor support (Form 990 Line 1h − Line 1e)
2. Government Grants — public-sector partnership (GOVGRANTS field)
3. Program Service Revenue — service demand and earned income (Form 990 Line 2g)

Section 4Data Collection & Sample Construction

Three data sources merged at the county level to create a cross-sectional dataset of immigrant-serving nonprofits in ICMA survey counties.

Data Sources

Nonprofit Data

IRS Form 990 E-filers (2019)

2,365 immigration-related nonprofits identified through manual review (ARNOVA classification; 2,382 initial set reduced by 17 EIN-based exclusions during deeper review). Financial data: total contributions, program revenue, government grants, assets, employees.

Policy Data

ICMA Local Government Survey (2018)

1,201 government responses across 849 unique counties. Measures immigrant support (8 items), economic support (4 items), enforcement activities (5 items), and NPO service provision (5 items).

Demographics

ACS & Election Returns

County-level controls: population, % foreign-born, median income, education, poverty (SES composite index), and 2016 Democratic vote share.

Identifying Immigration-Related Nonprofits

A two-stage process was used to construct the nonprofit sample from the universe of IRS Form 990 E-filers (2019).

Stage 1: Keyword Search

5,250 candidate organizations

A comprehensive keyword search was applied to the mission statements (MISSION field) of all Form 990 E-filers, targeting:

Immigration-related terms: immigrant, immigration, undocumented, refugee, asylum, DACA, migrant, naturalization, citizenship assistance, ESL, etc.

Ethnicity-related terms: Mexican, Latino/a, Hispanic, Asian, Haitian, Syrian, Somali, Vietnamese, Chinese, Korean, Arab, etc.

Stage 2: Manual Review

2,365 confirmed organizations

Two authors independently reviewed all 5,250 candidates to exclude false positives, yielding an initial set of 2,382 organizations. A subsequent deeper review excluded 17 additional EINs found to be inappropriate for the analytic sample, producing the final reviewed sample of 2,365. Exclusion criteria:

  • Geographic names mistaken for ethnicity (e.g., "Indiana," "New Mexico")
  • American Indian / Native American organizations
  • Veterans' support groups
  • International charities focused on populations outside the U.S.
  • Anchor institutions (hospitals, museums, universities)

Cook's Distance Influence Trimming

The largest organization in the sample (a Federally Qualified Health Center in Merced Co., CA) reports total revenue of $211M, with similarly large organizations dominating the upper tail. To prevent high-leverage observations from driving regression estimates, we computed Cook's distance separately for each of the three OLS baseline models (one per DV: ln(donations), ln(government grants), ln(program service revenue)) with the full right-hand side, then dropped the union of observations exceeding the threshold 4/n in any of the three models (n = 1,506; threshold ≈ 0.00266).

  • Step 1: Fit plain OLS (no cluster correction) for each DV on N = 1,506.
  • Step 2: Compute Cook's D for every observation in each of the three models.
  • Step 3: Flag observations with Cook's D > 4/n in any single model.
  • Step 4: Drop the union (deduplicated set) of all flagged observations.

Why the union (not the intersection)? An observation that is high-leverage for even one revenue stream still threatens the inferences for that stream. Using the union ensures all three models are estimated on the same trimmed sample, so coefficients are directly comparable across DVs.

Key Takeaways

1. Economic Support Complements, Not Crowds Out, Grants

Counties prioritizing immigrant economic inclusion direct ~37% more grant dollars per program to immigrant-serving nonprofits. The effect is concentrated on the extensive margin: economic-support policies widen access (whether NPOs receive any grants), not the amount per recipient. This stands against the crowding-out narrative in prior work.

2. NPO Centrality Shifts Revenue Away From Fees

Where nonprofits are recognized as primary providers, program service revenue is ~18% lower per unit increase. Consistent with the substitution side of Young's (2000) framework: when nonprofits are formalized as service providers, the revenue model shifts away from fees and toward contracted or donated delivery.

3. NPO's Role Conditions Policy Effects in Two Places

Grants (H4b): in NPO-heavy counties, the boost from economic-support policies is dampened among recipients (Econ × NPO = −0.109**, intensive margin). Prog rev (H4c): in NPO-heavy counties, broader immigrant-support policies are associated with even lower fee revenue (Imm × NPO = −0.071*). The supportive policy environment and the structural NPO role reinforce each other in reshaping the revenue mix.

4. Broad Carrot and Stick Measures Are Null

The eight-item immigrant-support index and the binary enforcement measure show no detectable main effects on any revenue stream. Local immigration policies, taken as broad bundles, may lack the salience needed to function as effective signals to nonprofit stakeholders — the signaling channel works for narrowly targeted instruments more than for diffuse policy bundles.

Analytical Approach Note

Why OLS-CR + Two-Part Hurdle?
With 36.7% singleton counties (only 1 NPO) and a median cluster size of 2, traditional multilevel (random-intercept) models cannot reliably estimate county-level variance. OLS with cluster-robust (CR1) standard errors provides valid inference without requiring large within-cluster samples. Two-part hurdle models complement the OLS specification for government grants and program service revenue, which have high zero rates (57.0% and 49.7%) and benefit from separating the participation margin (logit) from the intensity margin (OLS on recipients only). For donations, the 1.4% zero rate after Cook's distance trimming makes the hurdle Part 1 logistic under-identified, so OLS-CR is the primary specification.
